Dispute Timeline
Understanding the federal dispute process and key deadlines under the FCRA.
1
Day 0 — Mail Dispute Letters
Send dispute letters to all 3 bureaus via certified mail with return receipt. Keep your tracking numbers.
5
Day 5 — Bureau Receives Dispute
Bureau must begin investigation within 5 business days of receipt. The 30-day clock starts when they receive your letter.
30
Day 30 — Bureau Response Deadline
Under FCRA Section 611(a)(1), bureaus must complete their investigation and notify you within 30 days. If they don't respond, the disputed items should be deleted.
35
Day 35 — Send Follow-Up if No Response
If no response received, send a 30-day follow-up letter citing their failure to comply with FCRA timelines.
45
Day 45 — Request Method of Verification
If the bureau claims "verified" but provides no detail, demand the method of verification under FCRA Section 611(a)(6)(B)(iii).
60
Day 60 — Escalation Letter
Send a 60-day escalation letter citing inadequate response or partial compliance. Reference specific case law supporting your position.
90
Day 90 — Regulatory Complaints
File formal complaints with the CFPB (consumerfinance.gov/complaint), FTC, and your state Attorney General. This often triggers immediate bureau action.

Your Rights Under the FCRA
  • Right to dispute — You can dispute any inaccurate information directly with credit bureaus (FCRA Section 611)
  • 30-day investigation — Bureaus must investigate and respond within 30 days of receiving your dispute
  • Delete if unverified — If a bureau cannot verify disputed information, it must be deleted (Section 611(a)(5)(A))
  • Method of verification — You have the right to know HOW information was verified (Section 611(a)(6)(B)(iii))
  • 7-year reporting limit — Most negative items must be removed after 7 years from first delinquency (Section 605)
  • No re-aging — Debt collectors cannot reset the 7-year clock by selling or transferring the debt (Section 605(c))
  • Free annual report — You are entitled to one free credit report per year from each bureau at AnnualCreditReport.com
  • Damages for violations — You can sue for actual damages, statutory damages ($100-$1,000), and attorney's fees (Sections 616-617)
The Gilmore Dispute Order
Disputes are processed in a specific sequence designed to maximize impact.

Phase 1: Personal Information

Fix wrong addresses, misspelled names, incorrect SSN. Clean personal info prevents mixed files and strengthens all subsequent disputes.

Phase 2: Inquiries & Unknown Accounts

Remove unauthorized hard pulls and accounts you don't recognize. This addresses potential identity theft and reduces "new credit" impact.

Phase 3: Collections

Challenge collection accounts with debt validation demands. Many collectors cannot produce original documentation.

Phase 4: Charge-Offs & Aged Debt

Target old debts nearing or past the statute of limitations. Challenge re-aging and demand proof of original delinquency dates.

Phase 5: Late Payments

Address late payment records last. By this point, removed collections and charge-offs may have already improved payment history reporting.

Glossary
CRA
Credit Reporting Agency — Equifax, Experian, TransUnion. The companies that compile and sell your credit reports.
FCRA
Fair Credit Reporting Act — The federal law governing credit reporting accuracy and your rights to dispute.
FDCPA
Fair Debt Collection Practices Act — Federal law regulating how debt collectors can contact you and what they must prove.
Furnisher
Any entity that reports information to credit bureaus — banks, lenders, collection agencies.
e-OSCAR
Online System for Central Analysis and Reporting — The automated system bureaus use to communicate with furnishers during disputes. Often criticized for oversimplifying investigations.
MOV
Method of Verification — The procedure a bureau used to verify disputed information. You have a legal right to request this.
CFPB
Consumer Financial Protection Bureau — Federal agency that oversees credit bureaus. Filing a complaint often triggers immediate action.
SOL
Statute of Limitations — The time period during which a creditor can sue you for a debt. Varies by state (2-10 years).
DOFD
Date of First Delinquency — The date you first fell behind on payments. This starts the 7-year reporting clock.
Charge-Off
When a creditor writes off a debt as a loss. The debt doesn't disappear — it's often sold to a collection agency.
